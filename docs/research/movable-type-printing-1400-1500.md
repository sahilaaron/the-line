# The Development of Movable-Type Printing, 1400–1500

**Research file** · Compiled 2026-07-15 · Scope: the invention and European
spread of metal movable-type printing during the fifteenth century, with East
Asian precedents included as causal context.

## 1. Executive summary

Between roughly 1436 and 1455, the Mainz goldsmith **Johannes Gutenberg**
combined several pre-existing technologies — the screw press, oil-based ink, and
the idea of movable type — with a genuinely new one, the **adjustable hand mould
for casting metal type**, into a repeatable system for mass-producing books. His
**42-line Bible** (Mainz, completed 1454–1455) is the first major European book
printed with mass-produced metal movable type. Within roughly 45 years the
technology spread from Mainz to well over 200 European towns, producing on the
order of tens of millions of volumes (the *incunabula*) before the arbitrary
cut-off date of 1500.

Movable type itself was **not** Gutenberg's invention: ceramic (Bi Sheng,
China, c. 1040), wooden (Wang Zhen, c. 1298), and metal (Korea, by 1377) movable
type all predate him. His decisive contribution was the *casting system and the
integrated press-and-ink workflow* suited to a small alphabet. Whether he
invented independently of Asia, and exactly when and how his type was cast,
remain debated (§7).

## 3. People

- **Johannes Gutenberg** (c. 1400–1468, Mainz) — goldsmith; developed the metal-
  movable-type printing system and produced the 42-line Bible.
- **Johann Fust** (d. 1466) — Mainz financier; lent Gutenberg two ~800-guilder
  sums, sued him in 1455/56, and won control of the Bible workshop. With Schöffer
  he ran the first commercially successful printing house.
- **Peter Schöffer** (c. 1425–1503) — former Paris scribe/calligrapher; Gutenberg's/
  Fust's typographer; Fust's son-in-law. Co-produced the **1457 Mainz Psalter**;
  credited with early type design and the title page.
- **Andreas Dritzehn** — Strasbourg partner (from 1438); his death (Christmas 1438)
  triggered the 1439 lawsuit whose testimony exposed Gutenberg's press work.
- **Hans Riffe**, **Andreas Heilmann** — Gutenberg's other Strasbourg partners in
  the 1438 secret-art contract.
- **Arnold Gelthus** — Gutenberg's brother-in-law; 1448 loan, likely for equipment.
- **Archbishop Adolph von Nassau** — sacked Mainz (1462) and later (1465) granted
  Gutenberg a court pension.
- **Enea Silvio Piccolomini / Pope Pius II** — his March 1455 letter reporting Bible
  pages seen at Frankfurt is the key external dating evidence.
- **Albrecht Pfister** (Bamberg) — early printer of illustrated German books (c. 1461).
- **Sweynheym & Pannartz** — brought printing to Italy (Subiaco 1465, Rome 1467).
- **Johann von Speyer / Nikolaus Jenson / Aldus Manutius** — Venice; roman type,
  fine type design, and the octavo/italic revolution.
- **William Caxton** — first English printer (Westminster, 1476).
- **Anton Koberger** (Nuremberg) — large-scale printer/publisher (24 presses, ~100
  staff); the 1493 Nuremberg Chronicle.

### Claimed / disputed inventors (see §7)
- **Laurens Janszoon Coster** (Haarlem) — legendary Dutch rival claimant.
- **Procopius (Prokop) Waldvogel** (Avignon) — silversmith with 1444–46 contracts
  for "artificial writing" who left no printed books.
- **Bi Sheng, Wang Zhen** (China), and unnamed **Korean** metallurgists — the
  Asian precedents.

## 4. Inventions and technical components

Gutenberg's system was a *synthesis* of borrowed and new elements:

**Adapted / pre-existing:**
- **Screw press** — from wine/oil presses; Gutenberg re-engineered the platen to
  apply pressure evenly and with sudden release.
- **Movable type (concept)** — Chinese/Korean precedent (§7.4).
- **Codex** book format and **water-powered paper** manufacture (mills from 1282).

**Gutenberg's original / decisive contributions:**
- **Adjustable hand mould + matrix** — cast large numbers of identical, precisely
  sized metal letters quickly from a punched matrix. Widely called his single most
  ingenious invention.
- **Type-metal alloy** — lead + tin + antimony, low-melting and durable, better
  than earlier materials; the standard for centuries.
- **Oil-based ink** — adheres to metal type; more durable than water-based scribal ink.
- **Integrated workflow** — punch → matrix → mould → cast type → composed forme →
  inked → pressed, repeatable at scale. A type case held ~290 boxes (letters,
  ligatures, punctuation).

**Output (why it mattered):** a hand press could produce on the order of ~3,200–
3,600 impressions per working day, versus ~40 for a scribe copying by hand — a
roughly 100× productivity jump. (Figures vary by source and press generation.)

## 5. Causal connections

1. **Pre-existing pressure technology → the press.** Ubiquitous agricultural
   screw presses gave Gutenberg a ready mechanism; his innovation was adapting the
   platen, not inventing the press.
2. **Small alphabet → economic viability.** Movable type had existed in Asia for
   centuries but stayed marginal because logographic scripts need thousands of
   characters. Europe's ~26-letter alphabet made a complete, reusable type set
   cheap enough to pay off — the enabling condition for Gutenberg's system.
3. **Casting precision → mass production.** The hand mould/matrix produced uniform
   type at volume; without it, movable type could not scale. This is the pivot from
   "idea" to "industry."
4. **Capital (Fust) → the Bible; lawsuit → dispersal of know-how.** Fust's loans
   financed the Bible; the 1455/56 lawsuit transferred the workshop to Fust &
   Schöffer, commercializing printing. The 1462 sack of Mainz scattered trained
   printers across Europe, **accelerating** the technology's diffusion.
5. **Print → the "Printing Revolution."** Cheap, uniform texts underpinned the
   spread of literacy, the Reformation, the Scientific Revolution, and standardized
   scholarship — the long-run consequence historians emphasize.

## 6. Geographic spread (selected first-press dates)

Mainz 1452–55 · Bamberg c.1457 · Strasbourg 1460 · Cologne c.1465 · Subiaco 1465 ·
Rome 1467 · Augsburg 1468 · Basel 1468 · Nuremberg 1469 · Venice 1469 · Paris 1470 ·

Buda (Hungary) 1472 · Kraków (Poland) 1473 · Bruges 1473–74 · Westminster (England,
Caxton) 1476 · Odense (Denmark) 1482.

**By 1500:** presses in **200+ European towns** (sources give 236, ~250, or ~270);
total output somewhere between **~8 million and ~20 million** volumes across roughly
**30,000–40,000 editions** (figures disputed — see §7.5).

## 7. Disputed claims (with exact passages and sources)

### 7.1 When did Gutenberg actually invent printing? (1436 vs 1439 vs 1440 vs c.1450)
Sources give different anchor dates: partnership/"work began" **1436**; lawsuit
evidence **1439**; "perfected the secret" **1440**; Bible work **c. 1450–55**.
Wikipedia signals the uncertainty directly:

> "It is not clear when Gutenberg conceived the Bible project" and "It was in
> Strasbourg in 1440 that he is said to have perfected and unveiled the secret of
> printing."

Source: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg>

The **1439 Strasbourg lawsuit** is the earliest hard evidence of press work:

> "This document, which is rich in testimonies, subtly evokes printing elements
> such as forms, molten lead, punches and a press that was hastily dismantled."
> … "the court records refer to purchases of lead and printing materials — to
> forms and pieces that make sense only in terms of printing with movable type."

Sources: <https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?id=280> ·
<https://www.gutenberg-gesellschaft.de/en/johannes-gutenberg/gutenberg-in-strasbourg>

### 7.2 How was Gutenberg's type cast? (punch-and-matrix vs a "primitive" method)
> "Gutenberg's technique of making movable type remains unclear." … "Whether
> Gutenberg used this sophisticated technique or a somewhat primitive version has
> been the subject of considerable debate." … "If he used the punch and matrix
> approach, all his letters should have been nearly identical … The type used in
> Gutenberg's earliest work shows other variations." (2001 research.)

Source: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg>

### 7.3 The rival European claimants
**Laurens Janszoon Coster (Haarlem)** — said to have printed in the **1420s**,
per Hadrianus Junius's *Batavia* (written c. 1567, published 1588):

> Coster is "the purported inventor of a printing press from Haarlem" who
> "allegedly invented printing simultaneously with Johannes Gutenberg." …
> "There are no known works printed by Laurens." … "The earliest printed book
> from the Netherlands that has been dated with any certainty is from 1473."

The Gutenberg article's verdict: **"The Coster connection is today regarded as a
mere legend."**
Sources: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laurens_Janszoon_Coster> ·
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg>

**Procopius (Prokop) Waldvogel (Avignon), 1444–1446** — contracts for "artificial
writing" (*ars artificialiter scribendi*):

> Waldvogel agreed "to provide Davin de Caderousse with equipment for reproducing
> Hebrew texts," the contracts referencing "the steel double alphabet, along with
> the 48 types — one set made of tin and another of iron — and the 27 Hebrew
> letters." But "he did not print any books, and Gutenberg had his printing press
> probably already finished by 1440."

Source: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokop_Waldvogel>
*Significance: possession of type-like equipment ≠ demonstrated book printing; the
priority still rests with Gutenberg on surviving evidence.*

### 7.4 Asian priority and independent invention
Metal movable type predates Gutenberg by decades; the **Jikji** (Korea, **1377**)
is "the world's oldest metallic movable type-printed book"; **Bi Sheng** (990–1051)
"developed the first known movable-type system … around 1040 AD." Whether Gutenberg
invented **independently** is contested:

> Frances & Joseph Gies: "The Asian priority of invention movable type is now
> firmly established, and that Chinese-Korean technique, or a report of it traveled
> westward is almost certain."
>
> Joseph P. McDermott (counter): "No text indicates the presence or knowledge of
> any kind of Asian movable type or movable type imprint in Europe before 1450."

Source: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Movable_type>

### 7.5 Conflicting figures (Bible copies, dates, output, city counts)
- **Bible completion:** "the first finished copies were available in **1454 or
  1455**." (<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gutenberg_Bible>)
- **Bible copies:** "It is not known how many copies were printed, with the 1455
  letter citing sources for both **158 and 180** copies"; scholars estimate
  **160–185**, ~¾ paper, ~¼ vellum. **49** known to survive (2009), only **25**
  complete. (same URL)
- **Line count:** increased "from **40 to 42**, presumably to save paper" — hence
  the "42-line Bible." (same URL)
- **Total output by 1500:** one passage says "**220 printing presses** … had
  produced **8 million books**," while another gives "**236 towns**" and "**20
  million books**" for a European population of ~70 million; the *Printing press*
  article says "more than **twenty million copies**." Editions estimated at
  **30,000–40,000**. Cities with presses variously **236 / ~250 / ~270**.
  Sources: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_spread_of_the_printing_press> ·
  <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printing_press>
- **Press output rate:** ~**3,200–3,600 pages/day** (also stated as 240
  impressions/hour) vs ~**40** by hand — magnitude agreed, exact figures vary.
  Source: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printing_press>
